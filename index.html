Recipes that take you home

BY LISI LUDWIG

The Best Things in Life: Chocolate Milk & Popcorn

I'll be honest— chocolate milk and stovetop popcorn really do it for me.

Chocolate milk and popcorn carry the sentiments of the long and late nights in high school that I’d spend awake finishing school work. I liked those nights. The ones, much like the one right now (lol), where it’s an ungodly hour, but the world isn’t watching or barreling down on me.

If it was early enough where it was okay to make a little bit of noise but not late enough to where I’d feel bad waking my mom up, I’d go for the popcorn. If it was truly late, I’d sneak upstairs and conjure up my drink of choice from some Hershey’s syrup and leftover Lactaid.

The two “recipes” are simple but inspired me to ask others about the foods that bring them an equal amount of joy and nostalgia. I liked the idea of recipes that make you feel at home— both literally and figuratively. And so whala, I made a little thingy about that!

And in the best way, it feels extremely adequate and quaint that I again create and write this from the same childhood bedroom and kitchen that gave me those treasures to begin with.

The blunt of my efforts came through the interviews I conducted. Because while I love getting weird looks from my sister while I have a photoshoot with my chocolate milk, I love people and what they have to say too. Enjoy!

Conclusion

Bonus if you made it this far = more photos on the left from my chocolate milk and stovetop popcorn photoshoot.

Asking a broad array of people for their perspectives left me with the notion that food can be uniting and universal but also a individualized lens and context to who we are.

Put simply (and perhaps the best) Henrik and Lily enjoy food because its delicious and nutritious.

Both of them are too young to have invested decades in being part of a family recipe. Being so young also reminds us that sometimes we don’t know why we like what we like until we’re asked to think about it. We can rattle off cucumbers, tomatoes, chicken bakes but never acknowledge why those bring us joy.

There’s a perfect sanctity for me in that idea.

For Ernie, a torta ahogada is culture, work, and food. What struck me most here is that I had never imagined the recipes that make me feel at home as something negative. And while a torta ahogada isn’t necessarily negative for Ernie, it’s backstory and relevance is a connection with food I hadn’t realized possible or looked for in my life.

For Judy, Russ, & Gracie, food means family— whether the act of eating together, compiling favorite recipes for the family cookbook, or serving a dish that is a family staple. In their eyes, food is a source of stability— amongst the constant of change— as it roots itself in the people they love.
